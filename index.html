Module 01

What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a social engineering cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities — banks, colleagues, government agencies — to trick victims into revealing sensitive data or installing malware. It remains the #1 attack vector worldwide because it exploits human psychology, not just technology.

📧

Email Phishing

Mass-sent fraudulent emails

Hover to learn more

Email Phishing

  • Most common form — billions sent daily
  • Mimics banks, delivery services, HR
  • Goal: steal credentials or install malware
  • Often uses lookalike domains
  • May include malicious attachments
🎯

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted personal attacks

Hover to learn more

Spear Phishing

  • Uses your name, role, company
  • Researched via LinkedIn/social media
  • Success rate up to 70% vs 3% for bulk
  • Often targets executives (whaling)
  • Hardest to detect — very convincing
📱

Smishing

SMS & messaging attacks

Hover to learn more

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

  • Fake texts about packages, banks, prizes
  • Links lead to credential-harvesting sites
  • People click SMS links 8× more than email
  • Can also spread via WhatsApp, Telegram
  • Difficult to verify sender on mobile
📞

Vishing

Voice call deception

Hover to learn more

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

  • Caller ID spoofing makes it look real
  • Impersonates IRS, tech support, banks
  • AI voice cloning now used by attackers
  • Creates urgency to act immediately
  • Always call back on official numbers
🐋

Whaling

CEO & executive targeting

Hover to learn more

Whaling

  • Targets C-suite, directors, board members
  • Impersonates auditors, lawyers, regulators
  • Average loss per incident: $47,000+
  • Uses business email compromise (BEC)
  • May request wire transfers or W-2 data
🌐

Pharming

DNS hijacking redirects

Hover to learn more

Pharming

  • Redirects real URLs to fake sites
  • Manipulates DNS settings or hosts file
  • No suspicious email needed
  • HTTPS can still appear on fake site
  • Requires compromised router or malware
Module 02

Recognizing Phishing Emails & Fake Websites

⚠️ Real Phishing Email — Annotated
From: security@paypa1-alerts.com ⚠ Fake domain — 'paypa1' not 'paypal'
To: Dear Customer ⚠ Generic greeting — no name used
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be suspended! ⚠ Fear-based urgency tactic

Dear Customer,

We have detected unusual sign-in activity⚠ Vague threat, no specifics on your PayPal account. To avoid permanent suspension, you must verify your identity within 24 hours⚠ Artificial deadline creates panic.

Please click the link below to confirm your account:

http://paypal-secure-verify.login-now.ru/confirm⚠ .ru domain, not paypal.com

Failure to verify will result in permanent account closure and fund withholding.⚠ Threat of loss — pressure tactic

Regards,
PayPal Security Team⚠ No contact info, no real signature

🚩 Email Red Flags

✗
Mismatched sender domain

hover over the From address — does it match the real company?

✗
Generic salutation

"Dear Customer" instead of your actual name

✗
Urgency & threats

"Act now or your account will be deleted within 24 hours"

✗
Suspicious hyperlinks

Link text says one thing, actual URL goes elsewhere

✗
Unexpected attachments

.exe, .zip, .docm, .js files you didn't ask for

✗
Grammar & spelling errors

Legitimate companies proofread their communications

✗
Password/payment requests

No real company asks for credentials via email

✗
Too good to be true

"You've won $500!" or "Claim your free iPhone"

🌐 Fake Website Signs

❌ http://paypal.com.login.secure-verify.ru/account
✅ https://www.paypal.com/signin
❌ https://arnazon.com/deals
✅ https://www.amazon.com/deals
  • Always check the domain after https:// — ignore subdomains before it
  • Padlock ≠ safe. Attackers can get free SSL certificates too
  • Look for typosquatting: arnazon, g00gle, paypa1
  • Missing About/Contact pages or broken layouts
  • Login forms asking for SSN, PIN, or security questions unusually
Module 03

Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers don't just hack computers — they hack minds. These are the psychological levers they pull to bypass your critical thinking.

😨

Fear & Urgency

"Your account will be permanently deleted in 24 hours." Panic short-circuits rational thinking. When stressed, people skip verification steps and act impulsively — exactly what attackers count on.

"IRS Notice: You owe $3,200. Immediate payment required to avoid arrest."
🎁

Greed & Reward

Promises of free prizes, gift cards, or exclusive deals trigger excitement that overrides skepticism. The desire for gain is one of the oldest manipulation tools in existence.

"Congratulations! You've been selected for a $500 Amazon reward. Claim within 1 hour."
👔

Authority

Impersonating the CEO, IT department, IRS, or law enforcement creates a power imbalance. People are conditioned to comply with authority — attackers exploit this deeply ingrained behavior.

"Hi, this is John from IT. I need your login credentials to fix a critical server issue."
❤️

Trust & Familiarity

Using your name, your manager's name, your company logo — all researched from LinkedIn or social media — makes the attack feel personal and legitimate, lowering your guard significantly.

"Hey [Your Name], Sarah from accounting asked me to follow up on that invoice..."
🤝

Reciprocity & Helpfulness

People naturally want to help. Attackers pose as colleagues in need or offer "helpful" resources (fake updates, tools) that actually install malware. Helping feels good — attackers weaponize that.

"I'm locked out of the system for our 3pm presentation — can you reset my password?"
🕵️

Social Proof

"Everyone at your company already did this" or "Your colleagues approved this request." Herd mentality makes targets feel like non-compliance is abnormal or suspicious of them.

"All employees have already completed the mandatory security update — please do so now."
Module 04

The Phishing Attack Lifecycle

Understanding how an attack unfolds helps you spot and stop it at every stage.

1

Reconnaissance

Attacker harvests information about the target — name, email, job title, colleagues — from LinkedIn, company websites, data breaches, and social media. This takes minutes with free tools.

OSINT · Social Media · Data Breaches
2

Infrastructure Setup

Registers lookalike domain (e.g. paypa1.com), clones the target website's UI, sets up hosting, and configures email servers with SPF/DKIM to bypass spam filters.

Domain Spoofing · Web Cloning · Email Spoofing
3

Crafting the Lure

Creates a convincing email or message using the target's personal details, a compelling narrative (invoice, security alert, delivery), and an urgent call-to-action with a malicious link.

Pretexting · Social Engineering · Urgency
4

Delivery & Hook

Email sent. Victim sees a convincing message, clicks the link, and lands on a pixel-perfect fake login page — or downloads a malicious attachment that executes silently.

Email · SMS · Social Media · USB Drops
5

Credential Harvest / Malware Install

Victim enters credentials (captured immediately) or opens attachment (malware installs). Attacker may redirect to real site so victim suspects nothing while their data is stolen.

Keyloggers · RATs · Ransomware · Credential Theft
6

Exploitation & Lateral Movement

Stolen credentials used to access accounts, pivot inside the network, escalate privileges, exfiltrate data, or deploy ransomware. Average dwell time before detection: 207 days.

Account Takeover · Ransomware · Data Exfiltration
Module 05

Your Defense Playbook

🧠

Think Before You Click

Pause for 10 seconds before clicking any link or attachment. Ask: Was I expecting this? Does the sender make sense? Hover over links to preview the destination URL in your status bar.

Pro tip: Type URLs directly into your browser instead of clicking links
🔐

Enable MFA Everywhere

Multi-Factor Authentication stops 99.9% of account takeover attacks even when passwords are stolen. Use an authenticator app (not SMS when possible) for your most critical accounts.

Priority: email, banking, work accounts, password manager
📞

Verify Out-of-Band

Never verify using contact info provided in the suspicious message itself. Call the person or company back using an official number from their real website or your contacts list.

A 30-second phone call can prevent a $50,000 wire fraud
🔑

Use a Password Manager

Password managers autofill only on the exact correct domain. If you're on a fake site, the autofill won't trigger — a built-in anti-phishing mechanism that also prevents credential reuse.

Recommended: Bitwarden, 1Password, or your browser's built-in manager
🛡️

Keep Software Updated

Phishing attachments exploit known vulnerabilities in Office, PDF readers, and browsers. Keeping software patched closes these doors. Enable auto-updates where possible.

OS, browser, Office suite, and PDF reader are top priorities
🚨

Report & Don't Delete

Report phishing via your email client's built-in button or forward to your security team. Don't just delete — reporting feeds spam filters, helps colleagues, and may trigger an incident response.

US: report to phishing@apwg.org or reportphishing@antiphishing.org
📧

Check Email Headers

View "Show Original" or "View Source" to inspect SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authentication results. A failed SPF check on a "trusted" sender is a hard red flag.

Look for: SPF=pass, DKIM=pass, DMARC=pass — all three should align
🧩

Security Awareness Culture

Share phishing examples with your team. Run simulated phishing tests. Create a culture where it's safe to report mistakes without fear. One trained employee can save the whole organization.

Organizations with training reduce click-through rates by up to 72%

🆘 If You Think You've Been Phished — Act Fast

1 Disconnect from the network immediately
2 Change all compromised passwords from a clean device
3 Enable MFA on affected accounts right now
4 Report to your IT/security team within minutes
5 Run a full malware scan on the affected device
6 Monitor accounts for unauthorized activity for 30 days
